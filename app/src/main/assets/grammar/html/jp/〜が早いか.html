〜が早いか

Explanation in Original Language

〜するとすぐ続いて次のことが起こる。

Meaning and Nuances

The grammar point "〜が早いか" (ga hayai ka) expresses that something happens *immediately* after another action or event occurs, almost simultaneously. It emphasizes the very short interval between the two events, often conveying a sense of surprise or unexpectedness at how quickly the second event followed the first. The nuance is stronger than simply saying "after," highlighting the immediacy, as if the second event was waiting for the first one to finish. It's often used when the second action happens *so* fast that it seems like it happened at the same moment or even before the first one was fully completed.

Usage in Context

This structure is typically used in written language or slightly formal spoken Japanese, often found in narratives or descriptions of events. It connects two clauses, where the first clause is a verb in its dictionary form (or た-form in some variations, though dictionary form is standard) followed by が早いか, and the second clause describes the event that immediately follows. It is generally used to describe observable events and often involves a change of state or a sudden action. The subject of the second clause is often different from the first, and the second clause usually describes something non-volitional or an action not directly controlled by the subject of the first clause.

Structure: Verb (Dictionary Form) + が早いか + Second Clause

Examples

ベルが鳴るが早いか、生徒たちは教室を飛び出した。

As soon as the bell rang, the students rushed out of the classroom.

Describing the immediate reaction of students at the end of a school day.

彼はベッドに横になるが早いか、ぐうぐうと寝息を立て始めた。

No sooner had he lain down on the bed than he started snoring soundly.

Explaining how quickly someone fell asleep after lying down, emphasizing their exhaustion.

その知らせを聞くが早いか、彼女は顔面蒼白になった。

The moment she heard that news, she turned pale.

Describing an immediate, involuntary physical reaction to shocking news.

Comparison to Similar Structures

〜たとたん(に): Similar in meaning (as soon as), but often carries a stronger nuance of unexpectedness regarding the second event. The second event is often something surprising or unintended that happens right after the first.
〜か〜ないかのうちに: Emphasizes that the second action begins *just* as the first one is finishing, possibly even before it's fully complete. It highlights the minimal time gap even more strongly than が早いか. Example: 彼は起きるか起きないかのうちに、顔を洗いに行った。(He went to wash his face just as he was getting up / barely after getting up).
〜や否や (〜やいなや): Very similar to が早いか, expressing immediate succession. It is often considered slightly more formal or literary than が早いか.

Cultural and Practical Notes

This pattern is often used to emphasize speed and immediacy in storytelling or reporting events. Because it implies such a rapid sequence, it can sometimes sound abrupt or dramatic. It's generally not used for everyday, planned sequences of actions. Be mindful that the second clause usually describes something that happens almost automatically or uncontrollably as a result of the first action, rather than a deliberate, planned action.
