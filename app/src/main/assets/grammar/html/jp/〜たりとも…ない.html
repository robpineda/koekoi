〜たりとも…ない

Explanation in Original Language

１〜も…ない。全く…ない。

Meaning and Nuances

The grammar pattern 「〜たりとも…ない」 expresses a strong negation, meaning "not even one (unit of something)," or "absolutely no/none at all." It emphasizes the complete lack of something, often used with the smallest possible unit (like one second, one person, one yen, one drop) to highlight the totality of the prohibition or absence. It conveys a sense of strictness, prohibition, or absolute zero. This is a very formal and somewhat archaic expression, primarily used in written language or very formal speech.

Usage in Context

「〜たりとも…ない」 typically follows a noun representing a minimal unit, often combined with a counter (like 一人たりとも - hitori tari tomo - not even one person, 一瞬たりとも - isshun tari tomo - not even one moment, 一円たりとも - ichien tari tomo - noteyen one yen). The sentence always ends in a negative form (e.g., 〜ない, 〜できない, 〜許されない). It is predominantly found in written materials like regulations, warnings, formal notices, literary works, or in solemn pronouncements.

Examples

試験中は、一瞬たりとも油断してはならない。

During the exam, you must not let your guard down even for a single moment.

This sentence might appear in exam instructions, emphasizing the need for constant focus.

この計画のことは、部外者には一人たりとも漏らしてはいけない。

You must not leak any information about this plan to any outsiders whatsoever (not even one person).

A statement stressing the absolute secrecy required for a plan.

故郷を出てから、一日たりとも家族のことを忘れたことはない。

Since leaving my hometown, there hasn't been a single day I haven't thought about my family.

Expressing a strong, unwavering feeling or memory over time.

Comparison to Similar Structures

〜も...ない (mo...nai): The most basic form of negation ("not even..."). While similar, 「〜たりとも…ない」 is significantly more formal and emphatic, stressing the minimal unit and the totality of the negation much more strongly. Example: 「一円も持っていない」 (I don't have even one yen) vs 「一円たりとも無駄にできない」 (I cannot waste even a single yen - stronger, more formal).

〜ひとつとして...ない (hitotsu to shite...nai): Means "not even one," similar in strength and formality to 「〜たりとも…ない」. They are often interchangeable, though 「〜たりとも」 might be slightly more common with units of time or abstract concepts, while 「ひとつとして」 often applies more broadly to countable things. Example: 「証拠は何一つとして見つからなかった」 (Not a single piece of evidence was found).

Cultural and Practical Notes

This is a high-level grammar point (often JLPT N1). Due to its formality and strength, it's rarely used in everyday casual conversation. Using it inappropriately can make you sound overly dramatic or old-fashioned. It's most effective when conveying absolute rules, strong determination, or profound feelings in a formal context.
